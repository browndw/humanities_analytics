Coffee Break Experiment #1: From Curiosity to Visualization

Your first coffee break experiment is a low-stakes exploration: find something interesting in language data, visualize it, and share it with the class. This is about curiosity and quick discovery—not perfection.

This is a Coffee Break Experiment

You have limited time to: - Browse a corpus and notice something curious - Grab some data that might reveal a pattern - Sketch a visualization that shows what you found - Share it on one slide

Don’t overthink it. Pick something that catches your eye and run with it.

The Assignment

What you’ll create: One slide with a visualization and a claim

Where to explore: BYU English Language Corpora

Where to share: Class Google Slides (add your slide to the deck)

Key requirement: Include your name somewhere on the slide!

Part 1: Finding Something Interesting

Browsing the BYU Corpora

The BYU corpora give you access to massive language datasets. Here are some options:

Corpus of Contemporary American English (COCA): - 1 billion words (1990-2019) - Multiple genres: spoken, fiction, magazines, newspapers, academic - Good for: tracking recent changes, comparing genres

Corpus of Historical American English (COHA): - 400+ million words (1820s-2000s) - Good for: tracking change over time, historical patterns

Google Books Ngram Viewer: - Massive corpus across centuries - Good for: quick exploration, long-term trends

Start with Curiosity, Not a Hypothesis

Don’t feel like you need a research question. Instead, ask: - “I wonder if people use X differently than Y?” - “Has anyone noticed how often Z appears in news vs. fiction?” - “What happened to that old-fashioned word W?”

Explore first, explain later.

Exploration Strategies

Follow your interests: - Love science fiction? Compare “alien” vs “robot” over time - Into politics? Track “democracy” vs “freedom” across decades - Language nerd? Compare “literally” usage across genres

Look for patterns: - Change over time: Is a word increasing or declining? - Genre differences: Does a word appear more in spoken vs. academic texts? - Collocations: What words appear near your target word? - Variants: “email” vs “e-mail,” “OK” vs “okay”

Quick tips for the BYU interface:

  1. Search: Type a word in the search box
  2. Chart: Click “Chart” to see frequency over time
  3. Compare: Use the comparison feature for multiple words
  4. Download: Export data as CSV for plotting
You Don’t Need Big Claims

Good CBE findings: - “Huh, ‘literally’ spiked in the 2010s” - “Interesting—‘email’ overtook ‘e-mail’ around 2005” - “‘Robot’ appears way more in fiction than academic writing”

These observations are enough!

Part 2: Creating Your Visualization

The Assertion-Evidence Model

Your slide should have two parts:

  1. Assertion (headline): A clear claim about what you found
  2. Evidence (visualization): A graph that supports the claim

Watch this explanation (from 4:45): https://youtu.be/kbdO7adBRFE?t=280

Examples of assertion headlines:

✅ Good (specific claim): - “‘Literally’ has tripled in frequency since 2000” - “‘E-mail’ gave way to ‘email’ in the mid-2000s” - “Fiction uses ‘suddenly’ 5× more than academic writing”

❌ Too vague: - “Word frequency over time” - “Interesting patterns in COCA” - “Language change”

Choosing Your Plot Type

Match your plot to your data:

Time series (change over time): - Line chart: Shows trends clearly - Use when: tracking a word across decades

Comparison (differences between categories): - Bar chart: Compares discrete categories - Use when: comparing genres, word variants, or short time periods

Relationship (correlation between variables): - Scatter plot: Shows if two things co-vary - Use when: exploring if two words rise/fall together

Plot Types to Avoid for Beginners
  • Pie charts: Hard to compare precisely
  • 3D charts: Distort perception
  • Dual-axis charts: Confusing and easily misleading

Stick with simple line or bar charts for this exercise.

Design Principles: Less is More

Follow these quick rules for clear visualizations:

1. Clear labels - Axes: What are you measuring? Include units (e.g., “Frequency per million words”) - Title/Assertion: Your claim goes here, not “Figure 1” - Legend: If comparing multiple items, label them clearly

2. Reduce non-data ink - Remove unnecessary gridlines - Eliminate chart borders if they don’t add information - Use minimal colors (2-3 max)

3. Make it readable - Large enough font (18pt minimum for presentation slides) - High contrast (dark text on light background or vice versa) - Don’t crowd the slide—white space is good

Design resource: https://stat545.com/effective-graphs.html

Getting Your Data Ready

Don’t Use Screen Grabs!

Never take a screenshot of BYU corpus output and paste it into your slide. This creates a blurry, unprofessional image that can’t be edited.

Always work with the actual data to create your own chart.

Copying data from BYU corpora:

When you copy frequency data from BYU corpus results, it often includes hidden hyperlinks that will cause problems in Excel or Google Sheets.

Solution 1: Paste into text editor first 1. Copy data from BYU corpus 2. Paste into Notepad (Windows) or TextEdit (Mac) 3. Copy again from text editor 4. Now paste into Excel/Sheets—links removed!

Solution 2: Use Paste Special 1. Copy data from BYU corpus 2. In Excel/Sheets: Edit → Paste Special → Values only (or Ctrl/Cmd + Shift + V) 3. This pastes just the text, not the links

Creating Your Plot

Using Google Sheets (recommended for beginners):

  1. Enter your data: Type or paste (see tips above about removing links)

    Year    Frequency
1990    2.3
2000    3.1
2010    5.2
2020    6.8

  2. Select your data: Highlight both columns including headers

  3. Insert chart: Insert → Chart

  4. Choose chart type:

    • Chart editor → Setup → Chart type
    • Pick Line chart or Column chart

  5. Clean up the default design (this is important!):

    • Chart editor → Customize tab
    • Chart style: Uncheck “Border color” (removes box around chart)
    • Chart & axis titles:
      • Chart title: Enter your assertion (e.g., “‘Literally’ has tripled since 2000”)
      • Horizontal axis: Enter what X represents (e.g., “Year”)
      • Vertical axis: Enter what Y represents with units (e.g., “Frequency per million words”)
    • Gridlines:
      • Major gridlines → Uncheck (or set to light gray if you need them)
    • Legend:
      • If only one line/bar, uncheck “Legend” entirely (unnecessary clutter)
    • Series:
      • Increase line thickness (2-3pt) or make bars wider for better visibility

  6. Copy to Google Slides:

    • Click the three dots (⋮) on chart → Copy chart
    • In Google Slides: Ctrl/Cmd + V
    • When prompted, choose “Link to spreadsheet” or “Paste unlinked” (unlinked is fine for CBE)
Google Sheets Default Designs Need Help

The default chart has too much clutter: - ❌ Heavy gridlines that compete with your data - ❌ Unnecessary border box - ❌ Legend when you only have one data series - ❌ Thin lines that are hard to see

Spend 2 minutes cleaning these up—it makes a huge difference!

Using Excel:

  1. Enter your data: Type or paste (use Paste Special → Values if copying from BYU)

  2. Select data: Highlight both columns

  3. Insert chart: Insert tab → Chart type (Line or Column)

  4. Clean up the design:

    • Remove gridlines: Click chart → Plus icon (+) → Gridlines → Uncheck
    • Remove border: Click chart border → Delete or Format → Border → No line
    • Add axis titles: Plus icon (+) → Axis Titles → Check Primary Horizontal and Primary Vertical
      • Click each title to edit the text
    • Remove legend (if only one series): Plus icon (+) → Legend → Uncheck
    • Make lines thicker:
      • Click the line → Format Data Series → Line → Width (increase to 2-3pt)
    • Chart title: Click “Chart Title” and type your assertion

  5. Copy to Google Slides:

    • Right-click chart → Copy
    • In Google Slides → Paste
Why Remove Gridlines and Borders?

Every element that isn’t your actual data is “non-data ink.” Gridlines, borders, and heavy axis lines compete for attention with your main message. Your eye should go straight to the line or bars—everything else should fade into the background.

Compare: - Default Excel chart: Heavy grid, border, thick axes, small data - Cleaned chart: Just the data line, simple axes, clear labels

The second one is easier to read and more professional.

Using Python (if you’re comfortable):

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import pandas as pd

# Your data
years = [1990, 2000, 2010, 2020]
frequency = [2.3, 3.1, 5.2, 6.8]

# Create plot
plt.figure(figsize=(8, 5))
plt.plot(years, frequency, marker='o', linewidth=2)
plt.xlabel('Year', fontsize=14)
plt.ylabel('Frequency per million words', fontsize=14)
plt.title("'Literally' has tripled since 2000", fontsize=16, weight='bold')
plt.grid(False)
plt.savefig('literally_trend.png', dpi=300, bbox_inches='tight')
Good Enough Is Good Enough

Your first visualization doesn’t need to be perfect: - Colors not quite right? That’s okay - Legend slightly off? Don’t worry about it - Wish the line was thicker? Move on

Focus on: clear assertion + clean visualization that supports it

Part 3: Building Your Slide

Slide Layout

Top half: Your assertion (headline) - Font: 36-44pt, bold - Keep it to one line if possible (two max)

Bottom half: Your visualization - Make it large—fill most of the space - Leave some white space around edges

Corner: Your name - Font: 18-24pt - Top right or bottom right corner

Optional: Brief note about data source - Font: 14pt - Bottom of slide: “Data: COCA 1990-2020”

Example Slide Structure

┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ 'Literally' has tripled in frequency since 2000│
│                                                  │
│         [LARGE LINE CHART HERE]                  │
│                                                  │
│                                                  │
│ Data: COCA 1990-2020              Your Name     │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Adding Your Slide to the Deck

  1. Open the shared Google Slides: [Class presentation link]
  2. Add a new slide:
    • Go to the end of the deck
    • Slide → New Slide (or Ctrl/Cmd + M)
  3. Choose blank layout: Right-click → Apply Layout → Blank
  4. Add your assertion: Insert → Text Box
  5. Add your visualization:
    • From Excel: Copy chart → Paste
    • From file: Insert → Image → Upload from computer
  6. Add your name: Insert → Text Box → Type your name
  7. Check it: Present mode to see how it looks full-screen
Presenting Your Slide

Be ready to say in ~30 seconds: - “I noticed X in the BYU corpus” - “The chart shows Y pattern” - “I think this might mean Z” (or “I’m not sure what it means yet!”)

The goal is to share what you found and hear what others discovered.

Part 4: From Visualization to Interpretation

What Did You Find?

After creating your visualization, reflect:

Describe the pattern: - What does the visualization show? - Is there an increase, decrease, or difference? - How big is the change or difference?

Speculate about why: - What might explain this pattern? - Cultural shifts? Technology? Genre conventions? - Don’t worry about being certain—brainstorm possibilities

Wonder what’s next: - What would you look at if you had more time? - Other words to compare? Different time periods? More genres?

Embrace “I Don’t Know”

It’s completely fine if you: - Found something weird and don’t know why - Can’t explain the pattern - Have more questions than answers

CBEs are about exploration, not having all the answers. Share what puzzles you!

Example Reflection

What I found: “Email” overtook “e-mail” around 2005
Why it might matter: Shows how technology terms stabilize—the hyphen disappeared as email became commonplace
What I’d do next: Check when other tech terms lost hyphens (e-commerce? e-book?)
What puzzles me: Why 2005 specifically? Was there an influential style guide change?

Reflection Questions

After completing CBE #1:

  1. Discovery: What surprised you most when exploring the corpus? Did you find what you expected?

  2. Visualization: What was hardest about creating the chart—the technical steps, design decisions, or deciding what to show?

  3. Communication: Does your assertion + visualization combination tell a clear story? What would make it clearer?

  4. Curiosity: What new questions emerged from this exploration? Where would you go next?

  5. Tool comfort: How comfortable did you feel with the BYU interface and Excel/plotting tools? What would help you improve?

Quick Reference Checklist
